Uber

Present at the Hackathon was Sandeep Suresh of the Uber India growth team. As one of the sponsors, he told India Abroad that as evidenced by Uber CEO Travis Kalanick’s presence at the Start-Up India program, “We have a mentorship program and are happy to help hack- ers. Essentially, we want to see how they can help cities move better and help India move.”
He spoke of how Uber was constantly looking for inno- vations to connect. “There’re a lot of people in India or drivers who are not on Facebook and usually to advertise we use Google and Facebook ads. These drivers are not online, so we used the chaiwallahs to leverage people and refer drivers.”
The program provided incentives to those who would bring in other drivers and it helped Uber grow its network in India.
And with the typical jugaad innovation of India, the ini- tiative had even seen ‘Uber stores’ popping up “where they have drivers come in and teach them how to use the app and refer them to us. So there’s a lot of hustle going on and these are really street-smart people. They do not have technical knowledge but they are street smart.”

India

The event was keynoted by Ambassador Venkatesan Ashok, India’s Consul General in San Francisco, who talked about Digital India and what the Indian government was doing in terms of technology and startups.
‘Today crowdsourcing has become the gold standard for ideas and innovation,’ he said, noting that many compa- nies are born from events like this hackathon.’ ‘India today needs such innovative ideas to fast track development. The government of India is planning to spend about $17 billion in using technology to create a participative, transparent and responsive government.’
Later, talking to India Abroad Ambassador Ashok said he heard of hackathons for the first time during Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s visit to Silicon Valley last year; a hackathon was held at Google then. “That was pret- ty interesting,” he said.

“Technology can lift people out of poverty,” he said. “There’re a lot of problems in India today and we need to look how we can solve the issues through technology — like how to get peo- ple out of poverty, how to get them water, electricity, sanita- tion and health and education.”

And all of these are connected he said. “If there is a system where there is a provision of water, power, sanitation, then education and health are enabled… We will be able to transfer benefits from developments to poorer people much faster.”

He also spoke of how technology could be used to reduce corruption in India, citing the Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act scheme. “The payment which was given by cash from the district to the block and to the district village, now by opening zero-balance accounts, the money can flow directly into the farmers’ accounts,” he added. “So, now you don’t have this leakage problem or corruption problem and that is a huge benefit. There are many such technology enabled examples (that are) taking India ahead.”
